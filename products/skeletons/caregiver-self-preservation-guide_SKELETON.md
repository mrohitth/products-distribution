# PRODUCT SKELETON

---

## 1. PRODUCT TITLE

**Keep Who You Are: A Caregiver's Survival Guide**

---

## 2. EMOTIONAL HOOK (200 words)

The world tells caregivers to "practice self-care." Brush your teeth. Take a walk. You've probably tried. And you've probably noticed: that advice assumes you have margins. Assumes you're still a person with a life, and you just need a little maintenance.

You're not in that category anymore.

Maybe you're the adult child who moved back home, gave up your apartment, lost your friend group, and now can't remember the last time someone asked how *you're* doing. Maybe you're the one whose siblings "help" but only on their terms. Maybe you looked in the mirror last week and realized the person staring back has become a shadow — an escort, a coordinator, a bathroom attendant for someone else's life.

You already know what collapse feels like. You've been living inside it.

This guide isn't about spa days. It's about structural survival. It's about building systems that let you keep your job, your friends, your kids, your money, and your sanity — while still showing up for the person who needs you.

You'll learn to set boundaries that hold. To plan financially so one caregiving crisis doesn't ruin you forever. To preserve the parts of yourself that matter. To walk out of this chapter of your life as *you* — not a cautionary tale.

The people who emerge from caregiving intact didn't wing it. They built architecture. You'll learn how.

## 3. PRODUCT ARCHITECTURE

### PART ONE: RECLAIMING YOUR IDENTITY (Before the Hole Gets Deeper)

**Chapter 1: Audit What You've Already Lost**
*What you'll be able to do:* Conduct a honest inventory of the relationships, activities, and parts of your identity that have quietly disappeared since caregiving began. You'll complete a structured "Loss Inventory" worksheet that forces you to name specific things (that pottery class you quit, the coffee dates that stopped, the version of yourself who had plans). This isn't dwelling — it's diagnosis. You can't defend what you haven't named. By the end of this chapter, you'll have a concrete list of what's already gone and what's at risk next.

**Chapter 2: Codify Your Non-Negotiables**
*What you'll be able to do:* Define the three-to-five elements of your life that are so core to who you are that caregiving cannot consume them. You'll build a "Personal Constitution" — not vague intentions, but specific, named commitments. Example: "I will lift weights three times a week regardless of caregiving schedule. If this becomes impossible, that signals the system has failed." These become your tripwire — concrete markers that tell you when you've crossed from "sustainable" into "collapse territory" before you're already there.

**Chapter 3: Build Ritual Anchors Into Your Week**
*What you'll be able to do:* Design and schedule at least two recurring rituals that protect your sense of self no matter what caregiving demands arise. These aren't luxuries — they're structural load-bearing walls. You'll map these onto your actual calendar (not a fantasy calendar) and create contingency plans for when they get disrupted. Example: "Sunday morning solo coffee at the diner, 8-9am. If Dad has a crisis, this moves to Monday — it does not disappear."

---

### PART TWO: SOCIAL INFRASTRUCTURE (Rebuilding the Net That Should Have Caught You)

**Chapter 4: Conduct a Support Audit — Who's Actually There**
*What you'll be able to do:* Map your actual support network with unflinching clarity. You'll categorize everyone in your life into four tiers: "Present and reliable," "Present but unreliable," "Distant but willing," and "Not available." This reveals the brutal gap between the people who say "let me know if you need anything" and the people who actually show up. You'll identify which relationships need repair, which need pruning, and which deserve a direct ask (because they've been waiting for one).

**Chapter 5: Make One Specific Ask (Without Falling Apart)**
*What you'll be able to do:* Draft and send a specific, bounded request for help to at least one person in your support network. "Specific and bounded" is the operative — not "I don't know, maybe sometime, if it's not too much trouble." You'll use a script that names what you need, when you need it, and how long it will take. Example: "I need three hours next Saturday afternoon, 1-4pm, to do something that's not caregiving. Could you come sit with Mom? She'll be napping most of it." Most caregivers never make these asks because they can't bear the potential rejection. You'll learn to decouple the ask from the outcome.

**Chapter 6: Create a Rotation Schedule That Doesn't Depend on Goodwill**
*What you'll be able to do:* Build a formal, documented caregiving rotation that distributes responsibility across family members or paid support — so you're not the default 24/7 person. You'll use a templated schedule that assigns specific shifts, with backup protocols for when people cancel. You'll have scripts for pushing back on siblings who "help" on their terms. This isn't asking for fairness — it's engineering a system that survives without you being the single point of failure.

---

### PART THREE: FINANCIAL ARCHITECTURE (The Numbers That Will Ruin You If You Don't Face Them)

**Chapter 7: Calculate the Real Cost of Caregiving — Before It Bankrupts You**
*What you'll be able to do:* Run the actual numbers on what caregiving is costing you right now, and what it will cost over one, five, and ten years. This includes lost wages, foregone career advancement, out-of-pocket expenses, and the opportunity cost of your time. You'll complete a "Caregiving Cost Calculator" worksheet that forces you to be concrete (not vague estimates). Most caregivers have never done this — and the numbers are almost always worse than they assumed. Knowing the real cost is the prerequisite for making different decisions.

**Chapter 8: Access Every Financial Resource You're Not Using**
*What you'll be able to do:* Identify and apply for every financial resource available to your specific situation — state programs, Medicaid waivers, caregiver stipends, tax credits, veteran benefits, elder care grants, and insurance mechanisms you may not know exist. You'll use a checklist of 15+ resource categories with guidance on what each actually requires (the application language is often more accessible than caregivers assume). You'll leave this chapter having identified at least two resources you aren't currently accessing.

**Chapter 9: Protect Your Retirement — Even When It Feels Impossible**
*What you'll be able to do:* Build a minimal retirement protection plan that keeps your future self from paying the price for present caregiving choices. This includes understanding how caregiving impacts Social Security benefits, maintaining retirement contributions even at reduced levels, and setting one hard financial line you will not cross (example: "I will not cash out my retirement account for caregiving expenses"). You'll create a "Future Self Letter" that articulates what you owe the person you'll become — and the specific commitments that protect them.

---

### PART FOUR: BOUNDARY SYSTEMS (Learning to Say No Without Losing Everything)

**Chapter 10: Map the Boundary Violations You've Been Allowing**
*What you'll be able to do:* Identify and document the specific boundary violations that have become normal in your caregiving situation — the calls at midnight, the tasks you were volunteered for without being asked, the family members who dump and run. You'll use a structured worksheet that names each violation, what it costs you, and who benefits from the current arrangement. This isn't about blame — it's about seeing the system clearly so you can change it.

**Chapter 11: Build Scripts for the Three Conversations You Keep Avoiding**
*What you'll be able to do:* Write, rehearse, and deploy three specific boundary scripts: (1) the conversation with your caregiving recipient about what you will and will not do, (2) the conversation with siblings about redistribution of labor, and (3) the conversation with your employer about modified expectations. You'll have word-for-word language for each, adapted for common pushback. You'll practice with role-play scenarios. These scripts will feel uncomfortable at first — that's by design. You'll have them ready before you need them, not in the moment when you're already depleted.

**Chapter 12: Design a Sustainable Long-Term Plan — Not Just Survival**
*What you'll be able to do:* Build a 12-month caregiving sustainability plan that projects forward: What happens if your parent's care needs increase? What happens to your plan if you get sick? What does "done" even look like? You'll create contingency plans for three scenarios, identify the external support ( geriatric care manager, adult day care, paid in-home help) that would reduce your load before you hit crisis, and set a "red lines" document that specifies when you'll escalate, when you'll renegotiate, and when you'll exit. This chapter converts everything in the guide into an actionable, personalized system — not a to-do list, but an architecture.

## 4. KEY HUMAN TOUCH ELEMENTS

**The Touchstone Quote.** Open the guide (or the sales page) with the "bathroom attendant" language, adapted. Don't sanitize it. Caregivers recognize themselves in that framing immediately, and it signals: this guide knows what it actually feels like inside this. It won't waste their time with gentle reframes.

**Name the Failure Mode.** Early in the guide, name explicitly: "Self-care advice failed you. Not because you didn't try, but because it was the wrong framework entirely. You don't need a bath bomb. You need systems." This is the reframe that separates this guide from everything else they've read. It validates their experience with platitudes and then pivots hard to structural solutions.

**Specific Caregiver Language, Not Clinical Jargon.** Use terms caregivers actually use: "dump and run," "my brother who helps once a year," "the room that's not mine anymore." Don't use "loved one" (too diffuse) or "care recipient" (too institutional). The person writing this guide has been in the room, and it shows in the vocabulary.

**Acknowledge the Grief, Then Redirect.** There's a moment in Part One where you acknowledge that caregivers are often mourning multiple losses at once — the parent they're losing while that person is still alive, the life they had to pause, the future they can't plan for. This gets named, not fixed, and then the guide pivots: "Grief is legitimate. It's also not the only option available to you right now."

**The Exit Acknowledgment.** Include one section (likely in Part Four) that names what most guides won't: sometimes the right answer is to stop. Leave. Recalibrate. The guide should make clear that sustainable caregiving sometimes means reducing your role, not eliminating it, but that "I am done" is a valid answer. Many caregivers need explicit permission to consider this.
