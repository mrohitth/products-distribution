**Still Here: A Self-Guided Recovery for Caregivers**

## 2. EMOTIONAL HOOK

Some days, the weight of what you've taken on feels like it's burying you. You didn't volunteer for this — or maybe you did, once, and it looked nothing like this. Now you're managing medications, appointments, meltdowns, and grief that started months or years ago and hasn't stopped. Your own life has shrunk to a single function: keeping someone else alive.

The worst part isn't the work. It's the silence. The way people stop asking how you're doing because they don't want to hear it. The way you can't afford therapy — or can't find the hours — and the internet offers you tips about journaling and baths like those are real options. You are not alone in this. And you are not weak for feeling what you feel.

*Still Here* is a practical, self-guided recovery system built for people exactly like you — people in the caregiving trenches who need real tools, not inspiration. Over four parts, you'll learn to interrupt your burnout spiral, build boundaries that actually hold without guilt, construct micro-routines that restore your capacity bit by bit, and find a sustainable path forward — all without a therapist, without expensive programs, and without adding to your already-full plate. This guide meets you where you are. And it stays there with you.

## 3. PRODUCT ARCHITECTURE

### PART 1: INTERRUPT THE SPIRAL

**Chapter 1: Map Your Actual Depletion (Not Your Should-Be-Depletion)**

*You will be able to:* Complete a personalized Depletion Inventory that identifies your specific drain points — emotional, financial, physical, social. You'll see in black and white where your energy is actually going (often not where you think), creating a concrete starting point for recovery rather than vague "I need to rest."

**Chapter 2: Name the Invisible Tax**

*You will be able to:* Identify and articulate the three to five unacknowledged costs of your caregiving role — the canceled plans, the career slowdowns, the friendships that dissolved, the identity pieces you set down. You'll produce a written "Invisible Tax Statement" that externalizes these costs so they stop operating as ambient guilt and become addressable problems.

**Chapter 3: Build Your First Safety Signal**

*You will be able to:* Create and deploy a single, pre-committed "safety signal" — a brief action (a text to a specific person, a five-minute movement, leaving a room) that interrupts acute distress and buys you ten minutes before you react from burnout. You'll have this scripted and placed in three specific locations where you need it most.

---

### PART 2: BUILD BOUNDARIES THAT ACTUALLY HOLD

**Chapter 4: Calculate Your Real Capacity (It's Lower Than You Think)**

*You will be able to:* Complete a weekly capacity audit that accounts for sleep debt, emotional residue, and cognitive load — not just clock hours. You'll produce a realistic weekly map showing your actual available energy, which will immediately reveal where you've been overcommitting and why you're depleted.

**Chapter 5: Draft Boundary Statements Without Apologizing**

*You will be able to:* Write three specific, non-apologetic boundary statements for your three hardest interpersonal situations (family expectations, sibling dynamics, your care recipient's demands). You'll have language ready that is clear, honest, and doesn't require justification — because the impulse to over-explain is where most boundaries collapse.

**Chapter 6: Design a Permission Slip — For Yourself**

*You will be able to:* Create a written "Permission Slip" — a one-page document that explicitly authorizes you to rest, say no, leave, and prioritize yourself without earning it first. You'll post this somewhere visible and refer to it when guilt spikes. This is not self-indulgence. This is the operational manual for someone who has forgotten they matter too.

---

### PART 3: MICRO-STRUCTURE FOR RESTORATION

**Chapter 7: Engineer Three 10-Minute Reset Windows**

*You will be able to:* Identify and schedule three specific 10-minute windows in your week that are actually protected (not aspirational) and pre-loaded with one restoring activity. You'll map these into your calendar and have a backup plan for each, because the current approach of "I'll rest when I can" never produces rest.

**Chapter 8: Execute a Low-Effort Nutrition Recovery**

*You will be able to:* Implement three specific food changes that address caregiver burnout symptoms — stable blood sugar, hydration, reduced inflammatory foods — using pre-made options and meal assembly rather than cooking from scratch. You'll have a three-day starter plan ready to go, because the barrier is never knowing what to eat; it's having a plan that survives your exhaustion.

**Chapter 9: Install a "Future Self" Check-In**

*You will be able to:* Conduct a structured monthly "Future Self" check-in using a five-question written protocol that asks where you want to be in 90 days, what one small thing gets you closer, and what you're currently doing that moves you further away. You'll complete this with written outputs — not vague intentions — so you can track whether your daily choices align with your actual goals.

---

### PART 4: SUSTAINABLE HOPE — NOT JUST SURVIVAL

**Chapter 10: Reframe Your Role Identity (Without Quitting)**

*You will be able to:* Complete a role-identity reconstruction exercise that separates "what I'm doing" from "who I am." You'll identify which parts of your caregiving identity you want to keep and which are eroding your sense of self, and produce a written reframe that honors the role without making it your entire identity.

**Chapter 11: Find Your Unlost Self — The Interests You Set Down**

*You will be able to:* Complete an Interests Archaeology exercise: a structured inquiry into what you used to care about, enjoy, or want before caregiving became your primary identity. You'll identify one dormant interest to reactivate in the next 30 days with a specific, low-barrier first step — because most caregivers have lost themselves slowly and invisibly, and this begins the recovery of that ground.

**Chapter 12: Build a One-Page Care Plan That Includes You**

*You will be able to:* Write a one-page Care Plan that integrates your loved one's needs with your own restoration needs — not as an afterthought, but as a stated, non-negotiable component. This will be your reference document for healthcare teams, family members, and yourself. It makes your survival part of the caregiving plan, not a separate, guilty secret.

## 4. KEY HUMAN TOUCH ELEMENTS

**Anecdote that lands:** A specific story about a caregiver who tried to "power through" — what finally broke, what they learned, and how rebuilding slowly worked. The key is the messiness: not a polished recovery arc, but the real pattern of setback-then-adjust. This audience has zero patience for inspirational perfection.

**Framing that works:** "You are not bad at self-care. You are bad at self-care inside a system that doesn't support it." This reframe is critical — most caregivers carry guilt that they're failing at basic wellness, when the actual failure is structural. Acknowledging the system, not just the individual, is what builds trust here.

**What they need to hear (that they won't find elsewhere):** Permission to grieve the life they had before caregiving. Permission to feel angry. Permission to resent the person they love. This guide names these feelings directly and makes space for them — because suppression is part of what drives burnout, and most resources either avoid or moralize about this reality.

## 5. FORMAT & PRICING

**Format:** PDF with printable worksheets and exercises. Self-paced. No login required. Immediate access.

**Rationale:** Caregivers have unpredictable time — they need something they can pick up during a five-minute window and put down without losing progress. PDF allows that. No platform dependency means no "log in and remember" friction. Printables address the reality that many people in this audience do their deepest thinking on paper.

**Price point:** $37–$47

**Why:** This market is financially strained — they're choosing between groceries and therapy. Below $30 signals unreliability; above $47 creates hesitation. This range signals quality and seriousness while remaining accessible for someone who needs help now and can't wait for a sale. It's also below the cost of one therapy copay — which is the honest comparison point.

## 6. MARKET INTEL ANGLE

The caregiver burnout crisis is not emerging — it has arrived, and the infrastructure hasn't. An estimated 53 million Americans serve as unpaid family caregivers, a number that's grown by 20% in the last decade. The "sandwich generation" is now the "pressured generation." And the mental health system is not built for this population: therapy waitlists stretch to months; costs are prohibitive; and the episodic, crisis-driven nature of caregiving doesn't fit a 45-minute weekly session format.

What this audience has learned: they cannot rely on the system to rescue them. What this guide does: provides the structured support the system can't, at a price point that doesn't add to the financial stress already driving the burnout.
