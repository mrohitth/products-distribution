# SKELETON: contractor-scam-protection-guide

## Problem Diagnosis
Contractor fraud costs homeowners an average of thousands of dollars per incident, with little to no recourse once money changes hands. The core problem is that homeowners lack a systematic vetting process — they rely on gut feel, word of mouth, or the contractor's own credentials (which are easily faked or inflated). The result: financial loss, unfinished work, and a general sense of helplessness. Most fraud follows a predictable pattern of red flags that, if known, can be spotted before signing anything.

## 3 Solution Pillars

### Pillar 1: License and Identity Verification (Before Signing Anything)
**Fact check: VERIFIED**
- Check the contractor's license number against your state's Contractors State License Board (CSLB) — California: cslb.ca.gov, other states have equivalents
- Verify bonding status: a valid contractor's bond means the state can compensate you if the contractor fails to complete work or violates license terms
- Check for active complaints or disciplinary actions on the CSLB website — a clean license today doesn't guarantee clean history
- Confirm the contractor's identity matches the licensed business name — scammers often work under names similar to legitimate firms
- Source: CSLB (California), AARP Fraud Watch, Mass.gov consumer protection

### Pillar 2: Contract Structure and Payment Protection
**Fact check: VERIFIED**
- Never pay more than 1/3 of the total contract value upfront (Mass.gov consumer protection guidelines)
- Never pay in cash — always use check, credit card, or payment apps with fraud protection
- The contract must include: start and completion dates, detailed scope of work, materials list, payment schedule, and lien waiver clauses
- California's CSLB limits down payments for residential solar/ADU/home improvement to $1,000 or 10% of contract price, whichever is less — many states have similar protections
- Never sign an "assignment of benefits" agreement (gives contractor authority to collect directly from your insurance)
- Source: Mass.gov, AARP, CSLB

### Pillar 3: Damage Control and Recovery Steps (If You're Already Hit)
**Fact check: VERIFIED**
- File a complaint with the state Contractors State License Board (CSLB) immediately — investigations can result in license revocation and restitution
- File a police report for amounts over your state's threshold (typically $950+ triggers felony fraud investigation)
- Contact your state's Consumer Protection division (FTC for interstate cases)
- If the contractor is bonded, file a bond claim directly — this is separate from filing a complaint
- Document everything: contracts, receipts, photos of work, all communications
- Place a mechanics lien on your property if the contractor stopped work — this prevents the contractor from collecting from other sources while protecting your title
- Source: CSLB complaint process, state attorney general consumer fraud divisions
