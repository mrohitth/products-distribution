## Sudden Inter-Cat Aggression Guide

## 1. PRODUCT TITLE

**Why Is My Cat Attacking?**
*(27 characters)*

---

## 2. EMOTIONAL HOOK

You had two cats who tolerated each other. Then one afternoon, you hear it—a sound you've never heard before. Fur flying. Screaming. One cat has the other pinned and is tearing out fur.

You've separated them. They're calm now. Maybe they're even grooming each other again. But you're left with a question that won't let you sleep: *What just happened? Was that... normal?*

It's not normal. And you're right to be worried.

When longtime companion cats suddenly attack each other—really attack, not the日常 hissing that means "move over"—something in their world has shifted. It could be pain. It could be stress. It could be a change in your home that you didn't even notice.

This guide gives you the framework to find out what happened, what to do about it right now, and how to safely reintroduce your cats if the aggression is a one-off or the start of a pattern.

Because here's the truth veterinarians and behaviorists want you to know: **aggression without a trigger doesn't exist.** Cats don't "just become mean." They're telling you something's wrong—and this guide teaches you to listen.

## 3. PRODUCT ARCHITECTURE

### PART ONE: Medical First (Rule These Out Before Anything Else)

**Chapter 1: Use the Pain Checklist to Identify Medical Triggers**
→ You will complete a 15-item physical symptom checklist covering litter box avoidance, grooming changes, mobility shifts, and vocalization patterns. You will know whether your cat's sudden aggression likely has a medical root requiring veterinary investigation.

**Chapter 2: Differentiate Pain-Based vs. Redirected Aggression Using the Decision Tree**
→ You will use a visual flowchart to distinguish between aggression caused by illness/pain (vet visit required) and aggression triggered by external stimuli redirected at the other cat (behavioral intervention needed). You will stop treating the wrong problem.

**Chapter 3: Know When to Go to the Vet Today vs. This Week vs. Monitor**
→ You will have a clear three-tier decision framework with specific symptoms and timelines. You will stop Googling at 2am trying to figure out if your cat's behavior warrants an emergency visit or a regular appointment.

---

### PART TWO: Reading Your Home Environment

**Chapter 4: Complete a Resource Distribution Audit Using the Room Mapping Template**
→ You will map your home on a simple grid, marking every food bowl, water station, litter box, resting spot, and hiding place. You will identify resource deserts, single-points-of-failure, and whether your cats have to pass each other to access essentials.

**Chapter 5: Map Territory and Escape Routes to Find Control Points**
→ You will draw your home with your cats' natural territory zones and identify whether each cat has multiple high-ground escape routes. You will spot whether one cat is cornering the other in "neutral" spaces and understand why visual monitoring of approach angles matters.

**Chapter 6: Identify Hidden Environmental Stressors From the 22-Item Checklist**
→ You will run through a checklist of subtle triggers: new cleaning products,邻居 cats visible through windows, changed work schedules, moved furniture, new household members, scent changes (candles, air fresheners), and seasonal light shifts. You will identify which ones apply to your home.

---

### PART THREE: The Reintroduction Protocol (If Separation Was Needed)

**Chapter 7: Execute Three Scent-Swapping Methods in the Right Order**
→ You will perform three sequential protocols—blanket exchanges, dedicated swapping items, and full face-rubbing transfers—spaced over 3-5 days. Your cats will begin re-associating each other's scent as "normal" rather than "threat" before any visual contact occurs.

**Chapter 8: Set Up Controlled Visual Contact With Household Items**
→ You will build a sight-line barrier (door gap, baby gate with towel, screen) and position feeding stations at a distance that keeps your cats below their aggression threshold. You will recognize the body language that means "too close" vs. "curious but calm."

**Chapter 9: Run Your First Supervised Reintroduction Session and Know Exactly When to Abort**
→ You will conduct a timed (5-minute) neutral-space session with pre-positioned escape routes, carrying gloves, and a specific abort protocol. You will leave before your cats reach the "puffed tail and screaming" threshold—and you will know the three body language cues that mean "reset to previous step."

---

### PART FOUR: Long-Term Peace Infrastructure

**Chapter 10: Add Vertical Territory to Increase Perceived Space by 40%+**
→ You will use a simple shelf-placement guide to add cat-accessible vertical space along a natural traffic route. You will understand why cats who feel spatially crowded are more aggression-prone, even in large homes, and which three placements create the highest-value territory.

**Chapter 11: Build Resource Redundancy to Eliminate Competition Triggers**
→ You will set up multiple identical feeding stations, water sources, and resting spots in different zones. You will stop one-cat resource dominance (the "whoever gets there first wins" problem) by making every resource accessible from multiple approaches.
