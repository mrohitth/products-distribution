## 1. Emotional Hook (The First 400 Words)

This product opens with the exact paralysis the inheritor is feeling — not financial advice, not real estate strategy. The emotional core is: "I don't know what to do and I feel guilty about whatever choice I make."

**Structure:**
- Open with: *"Your grandmother's house has been sitting in your family for sixty years. Your grandfather refused to update anything. The roof is from 1987. The HVAC is from 2003. The wiring is a mystery that no electrician wants to touch. And now it's yours — not because anyone asked if you wanted it, but because death doesn't give you a choice."*
- The guilt reframe: *"Part of you feels weird about selling it. Another part of you knows you can't afford to fix it. And a third part has already spent three Saturdays at the property just sitting in the kitchen because it still smells like her. You're not懒. You're not bad at decisions. You're in the hardest decision of your life with zero preparation."*
- The promise: *"This playbook is going to help you make one of three decisions — sell, fix, or keep — using an actual framework, not just vibes. It includes a calculator that tells you whether fixing it makes financial sense. It includes a script for the 'I'm selling the family house' conversation with relatives who will have opinions. And it includes a list of every predatory "we buy ugly houses" company to avoid."*

**Tone rule:** Emotionally direct. Acknowledge the grief layer before the financial layer. The person is grieving AND dealing with a house. Both are true.

## 2. Part 1: The 48-Hour Assessment (What You Need to Know Before You Decide Anything)

**Core insight:** Most people start googling "should I sell my inherited house" before they've done any inspection. You need to know what you're dealing with before you can make a decision. This part is about information gathering, not decision making.

**Sections:**
- **The Five-System Quick Assessment** — A checklist for: Roof age/condition, HVAC functionality, Plumbing (look for galvanized pipes, check water pressure), Electrical panel (fuse vs. breaker, 100A vs. 200A), Foundation (basement cracks, water intrusion). Each item includes: what to look for, what it means, and whether it disqualifies you from a "quick sale."
- **The "Is This Structurally Sound?" Decision** — A simple framework: if the foundation is cracked, the house is a teardown. If the roof is gone, you need $15K+ before anything else. If the HVAC is original to the house and the house was built before 1985, assume $8K-$15K replacement. This assessment tells you whether "fix" is even on the table.
- **The 48-Hour Inspector Checklist** — How to find a home inspector fast (not for a sale, just for yourself). What to ask for in a "pre-sale inspection." Average cost: $300-$500 for a full report. Worth it before any decision is made.

**Human touch element:** *"I know what you're thinking: 'I can't afford an inspector right now.' I get it. But consider: the alternative is signing a contract with a cash buyer who will lowball you by $30K because 'the roof needs work.' A $400 inspector report is the cheapest insurance against losing tens of thousands in a bad sale."*

## 3. Part 2: The Decision Tree (Sell vs. Fix vs. Keep)

**Core insight:** Most people frame this as "sell or keep." But "fix" is a legitimate option if the math works. This section gives you the math, the emotional scoring, and a decision framework that accounts for both.

**Sections:**
- **The "Fix Math" Calculator** — How to estimate repair costs vs. market value. Rule of thumb: if repairs exceed 15% of after-repair market value, you're in teardown territory, not renovation territory. Include a simple spreadsheet template.
- **The Three-Question Decision Matrix:**
  1. *Can you afford the repairs without a loan?* (No = sell or cash-out sale)
  2. *Is the house in a neighborhood where renovated homes sell for significantly more?* (No = the math for fixing is bad)
  3. *Would keeping it cause more emotional distress than letting it go?* (This one matters — don't ignore it)
- **The "Quick Sale" vs. "Proper Sale" Breakdown** — What a quick cash sale actually nets you (typically 50-70% of market value). What a proper sale nets you after repairs. The math on why the quick sale almost always loses, except when you have zero cash to repair.
- **The "Sell to Family" Option** — Legitimate? Yes. Complicated? Very. How to structure a private sale to a family member without destroying the relationship. Hint: involve a real estate attorney.

**Coming in V2:** Detailed state-by-state probate sale rules and tax implications for inherited property (capital gains exclusions, timeline requirements).

## 4. Part 3: The Guilt Management Framework (The Emotional Part Nobody Talks About)

**Core insight:** The financial decision is half the problem. The other half is the guilt — about selling grandma's house, about not keeping it for the family, about the relative who thinks you're being selfish, about the family members who have opinions but aren't helping pay for the roof.

**Sections:**
- **The "What's Really Driving This?" Self-Assessment** — Six questions to determine if your "I should keep it" impulse is financial (bad reason) vs. emotional (valid, but needs a separate conversation from the financial decision).
- **The Family Meeting Script** — How to call the meeting, what to say, how to handle the uncle who will say "you're throwing away grandma's legacy." This is a real script, not vague advice.
- **The "Objection Responses" Cheat Sheet** — Exact responses to: "I thought we'd keep it in the family." / "You're just doing this for the money." / "I could help fix it up if you give me time." / "Mom would be so sad."
- **The Legacy Preservation Option** — If you do sell: how to preserve something of the house (a door hardware, a photograph, a piece of furniture) without keeping a house you can't afford.

## 5. Part 4: The Predatory Cash-Buyer Field Guide

**Core insight:** "We buy ugly houses" companies are not all the same. Some are legitimate. Some are predatory vultures who will take the house for 40 cents on the dollar and make you feel like you had no choice. This section tells you the difference.

**Sections:**
- **The "Is This Cash Buyer Legit?" Checklist** — Red flags: they won't show you comparable sales, they pressure you to sign within 24 hours, they ask for an "option fee," they don't have a local presence.
- **The "We Buy Ugly Houses" Pricing Framework** — What these companies actually pay: usually 50-70% of market value minus their repair budget (which they overestimate), minus their profit margin (30-40%), minus their holding costs. A house worth $250K will get an offer of $90K-$130K.
- **The "Fair Cash Offer" Alternative** — Local investment firms that pay 80-85% of market value. They exist. They're harder to find. This section tells you how to find them (hint: not through Google ads — through local real estate investor meetups and BiggerPockets forums).
- **The "Are You In a Position to Negotiate?" Assessment** — If the house needs significant repairs and you have no cash, your negotiating position is weak. This section tells you how to get the best offer in a weak position rather than accepting the first terrible offer.

## 8. Market Intel — Human Infrastructure Thesis

The inherited house crisis is a direct artifact of the collapse of multi-generational wealth transfer infrastructure. The house your grandparents bought for $12K in 1965 is now worth $350K, but the person inheriting it has a $65K salary and $8K in an emergency fund. The "gift" of the house is actually a liability — property taxes, maintenance, insurance — that the inheritor didn't budget for and may not be able to afford. The financial system has no product for this moment. Realtors want to sell, attorneys want to bill by the hour, and nobody is saying: "here's a $27 PDF that tells you what your actual options are."

Capital Pilot connection: This is the "human infrastructure" problem at the family wealth level. The products that will win in the next decade are not financial products — they're knowledge infrastructure products that help people navigate transitions that used to be handled by family elders, estate attorneys, and local community knowledge. The playbook fills the gap left when the grandmother who knew how to handle this died.
