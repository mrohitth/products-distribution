**STAY OR GO: THE RENOVATION DECISION CHECKLIST**

---

## EMOTIONAL HOOK

You're sitting at the kitchen table with a stack of YouTube renovation videos open on your laptop, a half-finished Prosciutto-wrapped Zillow search in another tab, and a growing pit in your stomach. You're 38, maybe 44, maybe 52—it doesn't matter. The math is the same. You're facing a six-figure decision that will reshape your daily life for the next three to seven years, and you cannot for the life of you figure out which direction is right.

This isn't about being indecisive. It's about the fact that no one actually teaches you how to make a decision like this. Your accountant doesn't have a framework. Your realtor has incentives. Your contractor wants the renovation. Your friends just say "follow your gut," which is useless when your gut is screaming contradictory things.

Here's what you actually need: a clear-eyed, structured way to run your specific numbers, examine your specific lifestyle trade-offs, and get honest with yourself about what you actually want from your home—not what you think you should want.

This checklist is that framework. By the time you're done working through it, you'll have a decision you can actually defend to yourself, a specific action plan, and a hell of a lot less anxiety.

## PRODUCT ARCHITECTURE

### PART 1: FINANCIAL REALITY CHECK

**Chapter 1: Calculate Your True Renovation Number**
You'll build a renovation budget from scratch using a realistic cost-per-square-foot framework adjusted for your zip code and project type. You'll account for the "allowance gap" (the difference between your optimistic budget and what things actually cost), and you'll produce a complete renovation cost estimate that includes contingency buffers. No more sticker shock mid-demo.

**Chapter 2: Count the Real Cost of Moving**
You'll itemize every moving expense beyond just the mortgage difference—agent commissions, closing costs, title insurance, moving trucks, potential short-term rent overlap, new furniture that the different floor plan requires, and the emotional cost of rebuilding your community. You'll produce a total moving cost sheet that goes beyond the obvious line items most people forget.

**Chapter 3: Run the Equity Math**
You'll map out your current home equity, remaining mortgage payoff timeline, and how much equity you'd burn on a purchase versus what you'd gain by staying and remodeling. You'll model two scenarios side by side: staying 10 years with a renovation vs. buying new and staying 10 years. You'll know, numerically, which path builds more net worth for your specific situation.

---

### PART 2: STRESS-TESTING THE RENOVATION PATH

**Chapter 4: Scope Your Renovation Reality**
You'll define exactly what "done" means for your home—not the Pinterest version, but the version that would actually satisfy you. You'll break down whether you need one room redone or a whole-house repositioning, and you'll understand why scope dramatically changes the risk profile of renovating versus moving.

**Chapter 5: Find Out What Your House Can Actually Handle**
You'll assess your home's structural limits—foundation issues, historic preservation requirements, HOA restrictions, and zoning that would prevent the additions or changes you're considering. You'll know before you commit whether your house can physically become the home you want, or whether you're fighting the structure itself.

**Chapter 6: Vet Your Contractor Before You Sign Anything**
You'll use the GC vetting framework—three reference checks with specific questions, proof of insurance and license verification, and a payment schedule structure that protects you. You'll understand why the cheapest GC is rarely the cheapest GC, and you'll know exactly what questions to ask before shaking hands on a project that could cost more than your car.

---

### PART 3: LIFESTYLE AND EMOTIONAL FACTORS

**Chapter 7: Map Your Next Five Years**
You'll project your lifestyle over the next half-decade—job changes, kid trajectories, aging parents, neighborhood preferences, walkability needs. You'll identify whether your renovation plan and your life plan are aligned, or whether you're renovating for who you were three years ago instead of who you're becoming. You'll have a clarity document showing whether staying or moving better supports your family's actual trajectory.

**Chapter 8: Name the Neighborhood Variables**
You'll assess what your neighborhood offers that's hard to replicate—the school district reputation, the commute, the walkability score, the neighbors you've spent years cultivating. You'll score your current neighborhood on dimensions that matter to you specifically, so you can honestly weigh whether "location" is a reason to stay or just a sunk cost you're protecting.

**Chapter 9: Face the Emotional Math**
You'll confront the sunk cost fallacy, the status quo bias, and the specific fear of regret that makes this decision feel so high-stakes. You'll name whether you're renovating because it excites you or because leaving feels like admitting defeat. You'll understand the difference between "I've already put so much into this house" and "I genuinely love this house"—and you'll know which one is driving your thinking.

---

### PART 4: THE DECISION FRAMEWORK

**Chapter 10: Build Your Weighted Decision Matrix**
You'll score each option—renovate vs. move—across your top six to eight weighted criteria (financial, lifestyle, emotional, timeline, risk). You'll produce a completed matrix that shows, numerically, which path wins on the dimensions that matter most to you specifically. You'll have a visual representation of your decision, not just a feeling.

**Chapter 11: Run the 5-Year Stress Test**
You'll apply a worst-case scenario to both paths—renovation delays and cost overruns vs. interest rate changes and inventory scarcity—and assess which path you'd regret less if things went sideways. You'll identify which decision has more optionality, and you'll know whether your personality type handles "change disruption" better than "living-in-a-construction-zone disruption."

**Chapter 12: Make the Call and Build the Action Plan**
You'll arrive at a defensible, specific decision with a clear action sequence. If you're staying, you'll have a prioritized renovation scope with a realistic budget and timeline. If you're moving, you'll have a target zip code, a pre-approval in hand, and a sell-first or buy-first strategy. You'll know exactly what to do next, this week, not "at some point."

## KEY HUMAN TOUCH ELEMENTS

**The Honest GC Story**: A real anecdote (framed as composite) about a couple who got three bids and chose the middle one, then lived through six months of delays, a sub-floor that had to be entirely rebuilt, and a final bill 40% over estimate—not because their GC was malicious, but because they didn't understand that " contingency" is a real number, not a courtesy. This grounds the contractor vetting chapter in actual consequence.

**The Neighborhood Attachment Trap**: The universal experience of staying in a home past its useful life because "we know everyone" or "the kids have been here so long." Naming this directly—as a real cognitive bias, not a judgment—helps readers get honest with themselves without feeling shame.

**The "Right-Sized" Kitchen Lie**: Most people think they need more square footage when they actually need better flow. The product should name that these aren't the same thing, because the renovation vs. move decision often hinges on whether a different layout could solve the problem without a bigger mortgage.

## MARKET INTEL ANGLE

This product sits at the intersection of two durable trends: the **financial anxiety of big-ticket decisions** (which spikes when housing markets shift) and the **desire for agency in life infrastructure choices**. Post-2020, homes became permanent infrastructure rather than temporary housing—people are deeply invested in getting their living situation right because remote work, hybrid work, and the collapse of "升官发财" (climbing the ladder and moving for it) have made the "stay or go" question genuinely existential.

The product also benefits from a structural trust problem: homeowners don't trust contractors, don't trust realtors, and don't trust their own gut. They want an independent framework that doesn't have a conflict of interest. A checklist that lays out the math honestly—including the costs where moving wins—is credible precisely because it doesn't need you to renovate. That's the competitive moat.
