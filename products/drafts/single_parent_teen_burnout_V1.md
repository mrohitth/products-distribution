## PART 1: THE ENERGY AUDIT — YOU FIRST, FOR ONCE

Here is the first thing nobody tells you about single parenting:

You cannot boundary-set your way out of exhaustion.

Every Instagram post about "setting healthy boundaries with your teenager" is written by someone who has a partner to split the load with. You do not have a partner. You have a job, a kid, a house, and roughly four hours of usable energy between the end of your workday and the moment you pass out.

Boundaries without bandwidth are just another thing you're failing at.

So before we talk about boundaries, we talk about energy.

### The "Where Did My Week Go" Worksheet

For the next 72 hours — this is a real exercise, not a thought experiment — you are going to log every hour from when you wake up to when you go to sleep. Use a grid, use an app, use a napkin. Just capture it.

When I did this exercise with clients in the past, the pattern was always the same: single parents were doing 6 to 8 tasks per week that their teenage children could handle but had never been taught to do. Not wouldn't. Hadn't been taught. Because the parent was too tired, too guilty, or too fast to just do it themselves rather than fight the battle.

You will see tasks on your log that are not your job. Laundry that your teen could do. Dishes that your teen could load. Groceries that your teen could help put away. The dinner you make every single night that your teen could cook once a week if you taught them.

You are not doing these things because you're bad at parenting. You're doing them because you've been operating at a calorie deficit for so long that "just handle it myself" became the path of least resistance.

**Key Takeaway:** Before you add one single boundary to your life, you need to know where your energy is going. The 72-hour log is not about judgment. It's about evidence. You cannot fix what you cannot see.

### The 20% Rule

Here's what I'm not going to tell you to do: delegate everything.

That's not realistic. It's not going to happen. And if you try it and fail, you'll add "failed at time management" to the pile of guilt you're already carrying.

Instead: in the next 7 days, I want you to offload 20% of your workload.

That's it. Twenty percent.

Here's how that might look in practice for a single parent:

- Your teen cooks dinner one night a week. Not "helps." Cooks. Pasta, tacos, a stir-fry — something they can handle. You supervise the first time. You are hands-off by the third time. If they burn one pan, you buy a new pan. The math still works in your favor.
- Your teen does one load of their own laundry per week. You show them the machine once. You are not their housekeeper. They are not a houseguest.
- Your teen handles one chore that you've been absorbing out of guilt or habit — dishes, trash, the bathroom mirror. Something. One thing.
- You sleep in on Saturday until 8:30. One morning. Your teen can make their own breakfast and not die.

The 20% rule is not about turning your teenager into a domestic servant. It's about the fact that you have been running a household that was designed for two adults, and you are one adult, and you are losing yourself in the process.

**Key Takeaway:** You do not need to overhaul your life. You need to move 20% of the weight off your shoulders. That's one dinner, one load of laundry, one morning of sleep. Do that first. Then we'll talk about everything else.

### The "No Shame Saturday" Protocol

This is the centerpiece of this guide. I'm going to be specific because I need you to actually do this, not just nod and move on.

Every Saturday, you block off 8:00 AM to 10:00 AM. You tell your teen — or don't tell your teen, your call — that you are unavailable. You lock your door if you have to. You put your phone on Do Not Disturb. You do not check the homework. You do not make breakfast. You do not answer "what's for breakfast" more than once.

Breakfast is cereal. The milk is on the counter. They will figure it out.

You are going to feel selfish about this for the first three weeks. Possibly the first month. Every time your teen knocks on the door or calls your name from downstairs, you're going to feel a jolt of guilt and the instinct to get up.

Fight the instinct. Stay where you are.

Here is what will happen: after about three weeks of consistent No Shame Saturdays, you will notice that you are not snapping at your kid on Sunday. You will notice that you have more patience for the homework fight that happens at 6 PM on Sunday. You will notice that the resentment you've been carrying — the "I do everything and nobody appreciates it" resentment — has quietly dissolved because you finally got one thing that was just for you.

**Key Takeaway:** The guilt you feel about taking time for yourself is not a signal that you're doing something wrong. It's a signal that you've been underinvesting in yourself for so long that any investment feels like abandonment. No Shame Saturday is not selfish. It's maintenance.

## PART 2: SCRIPTS THAT ACTUALLY WORK

The "No War" Method for Teenage Meltdowns

Most single parents default to one of two modes when their teen pushes back:

**Command mode:** "Do your homework now. End of discussion."
**Surrender mode:** "Fine, I'll do it myself."

Command mode creates resistance. Surrender mode creates entitlement. Neither works long-term.

The "No War" method is a third option: treat your teen like an almost-adult who gets a say, while still holding the line on what matters.

### The Teen Meltdown Protocol

When your teen is escalating — yelling, door-slamming, crying, or all three — you follow these three steps in order. Not all at once. In order.

**Step 1 — Name it.**
"You are really overwhelmed right now. That makes sense. School was hard today, your friend said something that upset you, and you're hungry. Of course you're overwhelmed."

Why this works: You're not fixing the problem. You're not minimizing it. You're telling your teen that their emotional state is valid and coherent. When a teenage brain hears "that makes sense," it actually calms down. The emotional flood starts to recede because someone acknowledged it exists.

**Step 2 — Pause it.**
"We are not solving this in the next ten minutes. We're going to let it settle. Let's circle back after dinner."

Why this works: When you're in a heated argument with your teen, you will say things you regret. They will say things they regret. The window of "we're both dysregulated right now" is not the window for problem-solving. Pause it. Eat dinner first. Come back to it when both of you are humans again.

**Step 3 — Boundary it.**
"You can be mad at me. You can be frustrated. You cannot call me names. Those are different things, and one of them is not okay."

Why this works: You are not telling your teen they can't feel what they're feeling. You are telling them that behavior has limits. Name-calling and verbal aggression are not acceptable, regardless of how valid the underlying emotion is. This is the boundary. Keep it simple. Keep it calm.

**Key Takeaway:** The Meltdown Protocol is not about winning the argument. It's about not making it worse. You will not solve the problem in the moment. You will prevent the problem from becoming a wound that lasts three days.

### "You Do You" Scripts

These are exact phrases that work. I have used them. Clients have used them. They are not magic — they are specific, and specificity is what makes them work.

**For homework refusal:**
"Your grades are your job, not mine. I'm not going to do your job for you. If you need help, I'm here. But the assignment is yours."

Why this works: You're not arguing about whether homework matters — you're drawing a clear line between their responsibility and yours. You will help if asked. You will not chase.

**For chore wars:**
"We both live in this house. I cooked dinner. You handle the dishes tonight. That's not punishment, that's just how households work. You pick the podcast or the music, I don't care which — I just need the dishes done."

Why this works: You're framing the chore as fairness, not punishment. You're giving them autonomy (pick the music) while holding the line on the outcome (dishes get done).

**For screen time standoffs:**
"Phone charges in the kitchen at 9:30. That's the rule. Mine too — I charge mine in the kitchen at the same time. We're both offline, we're both better for it."

Why this works: You're not being a hypocrite. You're not singling them out. You're making the rule about both of you, which removes the "you're controlling my life" dynamic.

**Key Takeaway:** These scripts work because they're specific, they're fair, and they don't require a fight. You say the words, you hold the line, you don't escalate. Practice makes this automatic.

### The Body Image Conversation

This section is primarily for mothers of teenage girls. It is the most difficult conversation you will have, and it is also the one that will most likely never come unless you initiate it.

Here is the script for the first "I hate my body" moment:

Do not say: "You look beautiful to me."
Do not say: "You're perfect the way you are."
Do not say: "Don't talk like that."

Here is what you do say:

"I hear that. That feeling is real — you're allowed to feel that way. Bodies go through weird changes and sometimes they don't feel like yours. Can you tell me more about what's making you feel that way?"

Why this works: You are not dismissing, you are not fixing, you are not invalidating. You are inviting more information and creating space for her to talk without fear of being shut down.

If she opens up: listen more than you talk. Your job is to keep the channel open, not to solve the problem in one conversation.

If she shuts down: "Okay. I'm here when you want to talk more about it. I love you."

The Body Image Conversation is not one talk. It's a standing invitation. You open the door. She decides when to walk through it.

**Key Takeaway:** The body image conversation is not about getting your daughter to feel better about her body. It's about getting her to feel like she can talk to you about difficult things without being dismissed or fixed. That channel, kept open through adolescence, is worth more than any specific outcome of any specific conversation.

## PART 3: THE IDENTITY RECOVERY — YOU'RE MORE THAN "MOM" (Coming in V2)

The Reddit poster called herself a servant. Not a parent. A servant.

That word choice is not incidental. It is the red flag that tells you everything about what is happening underneath the exhaustion: single parents lose themselves in the role. They become "Mom" or "Dad" and forget they were a person before they had kids. The role consumes the identity.

### The "Who Was I Before?" Exercise

Three questions. No judgment, no wrong answers. Just honest capture.

1. What did you love doing at 22?
2. What makes you lose track of time?
3. When was the last time someone asked about you — not your kid?

This exercise is not therapy. It is a permission slip. You are allowed to remember that you exist outside the role.

### The 15-Minute Reclamation

One tiny thing per day that is yours. A chapter of an audiobook in the car before pickup. Coffee on the porch at 6:30 AM before anyone wakes up. A 15-minute yoga video with the door closed. Not a "self-care routine." Just a daily reminder that you are a person, not a function.
