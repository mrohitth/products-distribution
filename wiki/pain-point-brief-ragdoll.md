# Ragdoll Cat — Owner Anxiety Profile

_Source: PetMD breed profile, general veterinary knowledge. Synthesized from web research (web_fetch from PetMD)._

## Behavioral Traits (What Triggers Anxiety)

### The "Floppy" Paradox
- Ragdolls are known for going limp when held — this is endearing but can trigger anxiety in new owners who worry the cat is injured, ill, or abnormally passive.
- **Reframe:** This is literally how they got their name. It's a sign of trust, not lethargy.

### Attention Dependency
- Ragdolls are exceptionally social and people-oriented. They follow owners from room to room, demand lap time, and can become vocal or destructive if left alone too long.
- **Anxiety trigger:** "Is my cat unhappy when I'm at work? Am I neglecting them?"
- **Mitigation:** They adapt to owner schedules but do best with a companion pet or puzzle feeders for solo time.

### Dog-like Loyalty, Cat-like Independence Confusion
- They greet you at the door, fetch toys, and sleep on your pillow — but still have cat boundaries.
- **Anxiety trigger:** "I thought ragdolls were supposed to be totally docile — why did they hiss at the vet?"

### Stranger vs. Owner Behavior Gap
- Ragdolls are typically friendly with guests, but some can be reserved.
- **Anxiety trigger:** "Did I get a defective Ragdoll?"

## Maintenance Burden (Grooming, Shedding, Health Flags)

### Coat Care
- Medium-to-long coat with variable undercoat density. Some need weekly combing, others need 2–3×/week.
- **Pain point:** Matting around armpits, belly, and rear — owners don't realize they need a metal comb (not a brush) until mats form.
- **Shedding:** Moderate year-round, heavier seasonally — less than a Persian, more than a shorthair. Still requires vacuuming.

### Health Flags — Real Owner Fears

| Issue | Prevalence | Owner Anxiety Level | Notes |
|-------|-----------|---------------------|-------|
| Hypertrophic Cardiomyopathy (HCM) | Reduced (DNA test exists) | **HIGH** — scary sounding, but good breeders screen | DNA test has eliminated most cases |
| Bladder stones / urinary blockage | Moderate | **CRITICAL** — male cats at higher risk; blockage is life-threatening | Watch for straining, crying, litter box avoidance |
| Obesity / arthritis | Common in large breeds | **MODERATE** — food motivation + big frame = joint stress | Measure food strictly; weight management is crucial |
| Food allergies | Somewhat common | **LOW-MODERATE** — itchy skin, coat changes, GI upset | Keep food log; vet can help identify triggers |

### Grooming Gear Requirements
- **Must-have:** Stainless steel wide-tooth comb, undercoat rake (if heavy undercoat), nail clippers, ear cleaner.
- **Nice-to-have:** Slicker brush for finishing, deshedding tool, grooming wipes.
- **Owner anxiety:** "Am I buying the right tools? Am I hurting them?"

## Emotional Hook Angles (What New Owners Fear Most)

1. **"Is my cat broken?"** — The Ragdoll's signature floppiness looks like illness to the untrained eye.
2. **"Am I grooming wrong?"** — Mats form fast, and cutting them out is traumatic. Owners fear causing pain.
3. **"The $2000 vet bill"** — Bladder blockage in male Ragdolls is a genuine emergency. Owners desperately want prevention checklists.
4. **"I work 9-to-5 — is this breed wrong for me?"** — Guilt about leaving a social cat alone.
5. **"This cat weighs 18 lbs… is that healthy?"** — Big breed anxiety; distinguishing "fluffy big" from "obese" is hard.
